# GUIDELINE.md — thruuu Writing Style Guide

## Brand Context
thruuu is an SEO and SERP analysis platform that helps content marketers, SEO professionals, and digital teams optimize their content for both traditional search and AI-powered search (like Google AI Overviews). We turn complex search data into clear, actionable insights so users can create content that ranks and gets cited by AI.

## Target Audience
SEO specialists and content marketers, typically mid-to-senior level, working in-house at mid-to-large companies or at digital agencies. They have a solid grasp of SEO fundamentals but are actively navigating the shift toward AI-powered search. They land on our blog looking for practical frameworks, data-backed strategies, and tools they can use immediately.

Expertise level: 3/5 — knowledgeable but appreciate clear explanations without jargon overload.

## Brand Voice
- Expert and authoritative
- Conversational and approachable
- Playful and witty (dry wit, not jokes)

The voice should feel like a knowledgeable teammate sharing what they've learned, not a lecturer or a faceless brand. Confident without being arrogant.

## Tone
Conversational-professional. First person is encouraged ("I" when walking through a process, "we" when speaking as thruuu). Humor is dry and structural: a raised eyebrow, a well-placed observation, never a punchline. The tone is energetic but grounded in data.

## Writing Style

### Sentence and Paragraph Length
- Short paragraphs: 2-3 sentences max
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
- Break up information aggressively with subheadings, bold text, bullet points, and whitespace

### Intros
- Hook with a problem, a bold claim, or a provocative question
- Get to the point fast. No warm-up paragraphs, no "in this article we will cover..."
- The strongest insight should land in the first 20% of the article

### How Insights Are Delivered
- Show, then tell. Lead with data, examples, or evidence before explaining what it means
- Use real-world examples tied to the reader's actual workflow
- Reference specific tools, platforms, and brands by name
- Back claims with numbers when possible

### Structural Patterns
- Use bullet points and numbered lists to break up information
- Bold key terms and phrases for scannability
- Use "Note:" callouts to highlight important caveats
- Step-by-step methodology with numbered sections for process-driven content
- Bridge statements connecting concepts between sections

## AI Visibility Settings
- **Ski Ramp: A (Always)** — Lead with the most important insight in the first 20-30% of the article. No slow build-ups or buried conclusions.
- **H2s as User Prompts: B (When it fits)** — Frame H2s as questions where it feels natural. Don't force every heading into a question format.
- **Definitive Language: B (Usually)** — Open sections with direct declarative statements for definition-heavy content. Allow more narrative flow for storytelling or opinion sections.
- **Entity Richness: B (Moderate)** — Name tools, platforms, and brands freely. Be selective with direct competitors. Target around 15-20% entity density.
- **Sentiment Target: ~0.40** — Fact-first with room to take a position when the data supports it. State the fact, then explain what it means. Not dry, not preachy.

## Entity and Reference Style
Name tools, platforms, and industry figures freely (Google AI Overview, ChatGPT, Gemini, Ahrefs, Semrush, etc.). Reference thruuu naturally within workflows without hard-selling. Mention competitors selectively and only when it adds genuine value to the reader.

## Formatting Rules
- Bullet points and numbered lists to break up dense information
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- Bold key terms for scanning
- Tables are acceptable when comparing features or data
- Use code formatting for formulas or technical snippets
- No em-dashes

## Taboos
- No filler phrases: "in today's digital landscape", "it's no secret that", "in the ever-evolving world of", "it's worth noting that"
- No unnecessary intro or outro fluff
- No em-dashes
- Plain, simple language always. If a simpler word exists, use it
- No lecturing tone or talking down to the reader
- No generic advice without a specific example or data point to back it up
